# Hook Templates — Substack Opening Lines

Proven formulas for the first 1-2 sentences. The hook determines whether someone stops scrolling or keeps going. Pick one, adapt it, make it yours.

## Rules
- The hook is a PROMISE. The article must deliver.
- Specific beats generic. Numbers, names, and details beat abstractions.
- One idea per hook. Don't cram.
- If it sounds like every other Substack, kill it.

---

## 1. Before/After
Show transformation. The reader wants to know: can I get that too?

- "[Person] used to [old painful state]. Now [new state]."
- "Six months ago, [X] took me [painful amount]. Last week it took [tiny amount]."
- "I rebuilt [system/process] from scratch. Here's what changed."

## 2. Stat Shock
A number that makes someone stop and think "wait, really?"

- "[Surprising %] of [things you'd expect to succeed] are [failing/abandoned/wrong]."
- "Only [small number]% of [large group] have figured out [important thing]."
- "[Big number] hours. That's how much [person/group] [wastes/saves] on [activity] every year."

## 3. Contrarian
Challenge what "everyone knows." The reader clicks to see if you can back it up.

- "Everyone's talking about [popular thing]. They're solving the wrong problem."
- "[Widely accepted idea] is wrong. Here's what [X years/evidence] taught me."
- "The [hot trend] crowd has it backwards."
- "Stop [thing everyone recommends]. It's making [problem] worse."

## 4. Confession
Vulnerability builds trust fast. Admit something real.

- "I made a mistake last week that [consequence]. Here's what I learned."
- "I used to believe [common view]. I was wrong, and it cost me [specific price]."
- "I almost [bad outcome]. One [small thing] saved me."

## 5. The Gap
Name the disconnect between what people expect and what's actually true.

- "[Impressive stat about capability]. [Disappointing stat about results]. The gap is [your thesis]."
- "We have [amazing tools]. We're using them like [embarrassing analogy]."
- "[Group] has access to [powerful thing]. [Tiny %] know how to use it."

## 6. Prediction
Take a position on the future. Readers click to see if they agree.

- "In [timeframe], every [role] will [surprising change]. Most aren't ready."
- "[Current trend] is about to [break/transform/collapse]. Here's why."
- "The [industry/role] you know is ending. What replaces it is [better/scarier/weirder]."

## 7. Question Flip
Ask a question that reframes the entire conversation.

- "What if the problem isn't [obvious thing]? What if it's [surprising thing]?"
- "Everyone's asking [common question]. The real question is [deeper question]."
- "Why does [common thing everyone does] almost never [work/deliver]?"

## 8. Specific Person
Name someone. Readers are drawn to stories about real people.

- "[Full name], [role] at [company], just [did surprising thing]."
- "[Person] told me something last week that I can't stop thinking about."
- "When [known person] says '[provocative quote],' you should listen. Here's why."

## 9. The Detail
One hyper-specific detail that makes the reader lean in.

- "It never sends a single message without his approval."
- "There's a file called `goals.yaml` that changes everything about how this works."
- "The 15-minute interval is what makes the whole system work."

## 10. The Cost
Show what's at stake. Loss aversion is stronger than gain seeking.

- "One wrong [action] — to a [high-stakes person] — and [months of value] evaporate."
- "[Organization] lost [specific amount] because [preventable mistake]."
- "The cost of getting this wrong isn't embarrassment. It's [real consequence]."

## 11. The Analogy
Compare the unfamiliar to something visceral.

- "[Complex AI concept] is basically [everyday thing everyone understands]."
- "Giving an AI agent full access without methodology is like [vivid dangerous analogy]."
- "We require licenses to [drive/practice medicine/fly planes]. But for [AI thing]? Nothing."

## 12. The List Tease
Promise a specific, countable insight.

- "Three things separate [successful group] from [failing group]."
- "I've [studied/built/reviewed] [number] of [things]. They all share [one pattern]."
- "There are [small number] reasons [big claim]. Most people only know [one/none]."

---

## Combining Hooks

The strongest hooks combine 2 patterns. Examples from our articles:

- **Before/After + Detail:** "Mike Murchison used to spend 90 minutes every morning processing his inbox. Now it takes five." + "It never sends a single message without his approval."
- **Stat Shock + Gap:** "42% of organizations are abandoning their AI initiatives. Not pausing. Not pivoting. Abandoning."
- **Contrarian + Cost:** "The 'just give it more autonomy' crowd has it backwards. One bad email and 500 hours of savings evaporate."

## Anti-Patterns

Don't use these. Ever.

- "In today's rapidly changing world..."
- "Have you ever wondered..."
- "Let me tell you a story..."
- "As we all know..."
- "[Buzzword] is transforming [buzzword]."
- Starting with a dictionary definition
